The AWI

The AWI used here is an amalgamation of the AWIs from each of the four countries. Notably, these use different methods of categorizing ancient woodland. I have attempted to unify these, based on my understanding of how the different categorizations. It gets a bit crazy1, hopefully this figure can help, or at least indicate the complexity of these datasets that some might initially think to be quite simple in their structure.

Ancient Semi-Natural Woodland (ASNW) is a subset of the AWI. It is defined as woodland that has been in existence since at least 1600 AD and has been influenced by human activities. ASNW is considered to be of high ecological value and is a priority habitat for conservation.

Plantation on Ancient Woodland Sites (PAWS) is a subset of the AWI. It is defined as an ancient woodland site, existing since 1600 AD, on which plantation woodland has been established. PAWS is a priority habitat for restoration.

Restored on Ancient Woodland Sites (RAWS) is a subset of the AWI. This woodland was formerly PAWS, but now has a canopy cover of at least 50% native species. It is important to note that RAWS is not a subset of ASNW, and that the process of restoration is ongoing on these sites: continued conservation management is needed for them to fulfill their ecological potential.

Roy is a sub-category of ancient woodland used in Scotland with evidence of existence in 1750, but with a potential of a breif unwooded interlude. Roy ancient woodlands appear on the 1750s Roy maps (providing historic evidence of woodland on sites extending beyond the maps on which other types of AWI are largely based), but may not appear as “woodland” on the c. 1850 1st edition OS. Many of these sites do however display many trees on the 1850s maps; the threshold of defining “woodland” on these is somewhat arbitrary. Size of ancient woodland sites

An ask came from Nick Phillips about the size of AWI sites, primarily to inform policy around PAWS restoration by acting as evidence RE that commercial viability of e.g. harvest operations at these sites, suspecting that many were small, unviable for commercial forestry, and thus commercial forestery concerns may be less relevant for policy. Here I sought to investigate that speculation. An additional ask was made around the proportion on the public

In particular we were interested in the percentage of sites that were < 3ha, which would essentially make them economically unviable for forestry operations.

Below are tables that summarise the site-area distributions of different AWI categories (high and detailed level), with info split by country. First, this is presented for sites (i.e. the number of sites distributed by their area) and then by (i.e. the combined area of sites of different areas). Obviously, while most sites may be small, more area can be within larger sites.

Notably, this is not related to habitat patch size, and should not be interpreted ecologically. Often AWI sites are contiguous with others, or other woodland that would effectively form part of the same habitat patch (while still being separate management units).
